In Gen the creation account begins broadly, and Gen 1 narrows the focus before Gen 1-3 frames the primeval history. Many readers start with Gen 1:1, though others cite Gen 1.1 or even the older style Gen 1,1. The opening movement may be given as Gen 1:1-3, Gen 1.1-3, or Gen 1,1-3, and a commentator might summarize the structure as Gen 1:1, 2, 3 or Gen 1:1-3, 5, 7-9. A longer discussion could move from Gen 1:20-2:4 into Gen 2:5-7; 3:1-6, and then compare Gen 3:15 with 3:15b or 3:15a-b once the book is implied by context. Some writers cite Gen 1:1ff, others Gen 1:1 ff, and others Gen 1:1f.

In a study of the patriarchal narratives, one might begin with Genesis 12, then narrow to Genesis 12:1, 12:1-3, 12:4, 7, 10, or 12:1-3, 4-5, 10-12. The same paragraph may continue with Gen 13; 15; 17, or with Gen 13-17 if the scope is broad. A careful note could read: see Gen 15:1; 17:1; and 22:2. Another might say cf. Gen 15:6; 17:5; 22:17. Parenthetical references also occur naturally (Gen 12:1-3), (Gen 15:6; 22:18), and [Gen 17:1].

When the book is already established, references may appear simply as 1:1, 1:1-3, 1:1, 3, 5, 1:1-3, 5, 7-9, 1:20-2:4, 2:1; 3:4; and 4:1-8. In looser prose a writer may refer only to chapter 1, chapters 1-3, ch 1, ch. 1, chap 1, chap. 1, chs 1-3, or chs. 1-3, 5. Likewise, verse references can appear as verse 2, verses 2-4, v 2, v. 2, vv 2-4, vv. 2-4, vs 2-4, or vs. 2-4. Combined trigger forms are common too: chapter 1 verse 2, chapter 1 verses 2-4, ch 1 v 2, ch. 1 v. 2, ch 1 vv 2-4, and ch. 1 vv. 2-4.

Exodus is cited as Exod 3, Exod 3:2, Exod 3:2-6, Exod 3:2, 4, 6, Exod 3:2-6, 8-10, and Exod 3:2-4:3. A sermon note may say Exod 3:2; 4:1; 6:1, while a comparative note may read Gen 1:1; Exod 3:2; John 1:1. Some styles abbreviate more tightly: Exod3:2 or John1:1. Others preserve space but use dots: John 1.1-5. In some European conventions one even sees John 1,1-5.

The Psalms generate their own patterns: Ps 23, Ps 23:1, Psalm 23, Psalm 23:1-3, Pss 1-2, and Ps 23:1, 4, 6. A textual note may mention Ps 3 title or Ps 3 superscription. Another may compare Ps 51:1 (Eng 51:3) or Ps 3:1 (Eng 3:0), reflecting alternate versification systems.

Numbered books vary considerably. A text may cite 1 Sam 3:1, 1Sam 3:1, 1 Sam. 3:1, 1 Samuel 3:1, I Samuel 3:1, First Samuel 3:1, or 1st Samuel 3:1. Likewise one finds 2 Kings 2:1, II Kings 2:1, Second Kings 2:1, or 2nd Kings 2:1. In the New Testament the same variation appears in 1 John 1:1, I John 1:1, First John 1:1, 1st John 1:1, 2 John 1:6, II John 1:6, and 3 John 1:2, III John 1:2, Third John 1:2.

Some books carry multiple conventional names. Song 1:1 may also appear as Song of Songs 1:1, Song of Solomon 1:1, or Cant 1:1. The same is true for Canticles 1:1. References to the prophets may alternate between Isa 6:1-8 and Isaiah 6:1-8; Jer 31:31-34 and Jeremiah 31:31-34; Ezek 37:1-14 and Ezekiel 37:1-14.

Lists often become quite dense. A critical apparatus might read Rom 5:1-5, 8, 10-11; 6:1-4; 8:1, 14-17, 28-30. Another sentence could say see also Matt 5:3-12; Luke 6:20-23; and Jas 2:5. Or a writer may compress the same material as Matt 5; 7; Luke 6. At times the book is not repeated after a semicolon when context is clear: Matt 5:3-12; 6:9-13; 7:1-5.

Verse suffixes appear especially in close reading: Mark 14:41a, 14:41b, 14:41a-b, and sometimes 14:41b-42. A detailed note might distinguish John 1:18a from John 1:18b. Likewise, a commentary may cite Isa 52:13-53:12, then discuss 53:4b separately.

References can be embedded in ordinary prose: In Genesis 1:1 the narrative opens with divine initiative, while in John 1:1 the Logos is already present. Compare also Rom 8:28, which some link to 8:29-30, and cf. Phil 2:6-11. One writer may say “as stated in Gen 12:1-3,” another “see Gen 12.1-3,” and another “see Gen 12,1-3.” Parenthetical style remains common: Abraham’s call (Gen 12:1-3) anticipates later promise (15:1-6; 17:1-8; 22:15-18).

Chapter-only references can also be embedded naturally: the argument in Romans chapter 8 differs from that in chapter 7; compare ch. 8 with chs. 9-11. Or again: the warning in Matthew chapters 5-7 culminates in chapter 7. Verse-only references can be similarly contextual: in verse 3 the emphasis shifts; verses 4-6 elaborate; vv. 7-9 conclude the unit; and v. 10 provides the transition.

Sometimes conjunctions are carried into the citation itself: Gen 1:5; 4:4; and 6:1. The same occurs without the book when context supplies it: 1:5; 4:4; and 6:1. One also sees leading discourse markers such as cf. 1:3-5, see 2:1-4, see also 3:1-6, compare 4:1-8, or and 5:1 if the previous sentence has already established the book.

A heavily mixed citation may therefore read: Gen 1:1-3, 5, 7-9; 2:4-7; 3:1, 4, 8-10; cf. Exod 3:2-6; Ps 8:4-6; Isa 40:1-5; John 1:1-5; Rom 8:28-30; and Rev 21:1-4. Another mixed example with implied context could read: 1:1-3, 5; 2:4; 3:1-6; 3:15a-b; 6:1ff.

For trigger-word testing specifically, include these prose forms as well: chapter 3, chapters 3-5, ch 3, ch. 3, chap 3, chap. 3, chs 3-5, chs. 3-5, 7; verse 2, verses 2-4, v 2, v. 2, vv 2-4, vv. 2-4, vs 2-4, vs. 2-4; chapter 3 verse 2, chapter 3 verses 2-4, ch 3 v 2, ch 3 v. 2, ch. 3 vv. 2-4, and ch 3:2-4.

Finally, the same source may contain book-only references (Gen, Exodus, Ps, John), chapter-only references (Gen 1, John 3), chapter ranges (Gen 1-3, Rom 9-11), single verses (Gen 1:2), dotted verses (Gen 1.2), comma verses (Gen 1,2), verse ranges (Gen 1:2-4), verse lists (Gen 1:1, 2, 3), verse lists with ranges (Gen 31:1-8, 14-15, 23), semicolon-separated references (Gen 1:1-3; 2:4), cross-chapter ranges (Gen 1:20-2:4), verse suffixes (Gen 1:3a), and optional conjunctions (Gen 1:5; 4:4; and 6:1).
